Euler’s Totient Function

Definition

The Euler’s Totient Function, denoted as ϕ(n)\phi(n)ϕ(n), is defined as the number of positive integers less than or equal to nnn that are relatively prime to nnn.

Properties

Property 1. ϕ(n)=n−1\phi(n) = n - 1ϕ(n)=n−1, if nnn is prime

Proof

It’s easy to see that all numbers less than nnn are relatively prime to nnn if nnn is prime.

Therefore, ϕ(n)=n−1\phi(n) = n - 1ϕ(n)=n−1.

□\square□

Property 2. ϕ(pq)=(p−1)(q−1)\phi(pq) = (p - 1)(q - 1)ϕ(pq)=(p−1)(q−1), if ppp and qqq are distinct primes

Proof

Since ppp is prime, gcd⁡(p,k)≠1\operatorname{gcd}(p, k) \neq 1gcd(p,k)=1 gives p∣kp | kp∣k. Hence, numbers less than pqpqpq that are not relatively prime to pqpqpq are p,2p,…,(q−1)pp, 2p, \dots, (q - 1)pp,2p,…,(q−1)p. It’s easy to see that there are q−1q - 1q−1 such numbers.

Similarly, there are p−1p - 1p−1 numbers less than pqpqpq that are not relatively prime to pqpqpq and the union of these 2 sets is empty since there is no such xxx that satisfy p∣xp | xp∣x, q∣xq | xq∣x and x<pqx < pqx<pq.

Therefore, ϕ(pq)=pq−1−(p−1)−(q−1)=(p−1)(q−1)\phi(pq) = pq - 1 - (p - 1) - (q - 1) = (p - 1)(q - 1)ϕ(pq)=pq−1−(p−1)−(q−1)=(p−1)(q−1).

□\square□

Property 3. ϕ(pk)=pk−pk−1\phi(p^k) = p^k - p^{k - 1}ϕ(pk)=pk−pk−1, if ppp is prime

Proof

Since ppp is prime, gcd⁡(p,k)≠1\operatorname{gcd}(p, k) \neq 1gcd(p,k)=1 gives p∣kp | kp∣k.

Hence, numbers less than pkp^kpk that are not relatively prime to pkp^kpk are p,2p,…,(pk−1−1)pp, 2p, \dots, (p^{k - 1} - 1)pp,2p,…,(pk−1−1)p. It’s easy to see that there are ⌊pkp⌋−1=pk−1−1\lfloor \dfrac{p ^{k}}{p} \rfloor - 1 = p^{k - 1} - 1⌊ppk​⌋−1=pk−1−1 such numbers.

Therefore, ϕ(pk)=pk−1−(pk−1−1)=pk−pk−1\phi(p^k) = p^k - 1 - (p^{k - 1} - 1) = p^k - p^{k - 1}ϕ(pk)=pk−1−(pk−1−1)=pk−pk−1.

we know such xxx is also coprime to ababab.

It’s easy to see echo xxx which is coprime to a,ba, ba,b can be mapped to a unique pair of numbers (m,n)(m, n)(m,n) that is coprime to ababab s.t 0≤m<a0 \leq m < a0≤m<a and 0≤n<b0 \leq n < b0≤n<b.

Therefore, there are ϕ(a)ϕ(b)\phi(a)\phi(b)ϕ(a)ϕ(b) numbers less than ababab that are coprime to ababab.

□\square□

Euler’s Theorem

Euler’s Theorem states that if aaa and nnn are coprime positive integers, then

aϕ(n)≡1(modn)a^{\phi(n)} \equiv 1 \pmod{n}aϕ(n)≡1(modn)

Proof

Let B={b1,b2,…,bϕ(n)}B = \{b_1, b_2, \dots, b_{\phi(n)}\}B={b1​,b2​,…,bϕ(n)​} be the set of numbers less than nnn that are coprime to nnn.

Assume that there are two such numbers bib_ibi​ and bjb_jbj​ such that abi≡abj(modn)a b_i \equiv a b_j \pmod{n}abi​≡abj​(modn) and bi≠bjb_i \neq b_jbi​=bj​.

abi≡abj(modn)abi−abj≡0(modn)a(bi−bj)≡0(modn)\begin{gather*} a b_i &\equiv a b_j \pmod{n} \\ a b_i - a b_j &\equiv 0 \pmod{n} \\ a (b_i - b_j) &\equiv 0 \pmod{n} \end{gather*}abi​abi​−abj​a(bi​−bj​)​≡abj​(modn)≡0(modn)≡0(modn)​

Since aaa and nnn are coprime, n∣a(bi−bj)n | a (b_i - b_j)n∣a(bi​−bj​) gives n∣bi−bjn | b_i - b_jn∣bi​−bj​ or bi≡bj(modn)b_i \equiv b_j \pmod{n}bi​≡bj​(modn), which contradicts with the assumption that bi≠bjb_i \neq b_jbi​=bj​.

What’s more, since both aaa and bib_ibi​ are coprime to nnn, abia b_iabi​ is also coprime to nnn.

Therefore, {ab1,ab2,…,abϕ(n)}\{a b_1, a b_2, \dots, a b_{\phi(n)}\}{ab1​,ab2​,…,abϕ(n)​} is a permutation of BBB.

Hence,

∏i=1ϕ(n)abi≡∏i=1ϕ(n)bi(modn)aϕ(n)∏i=1ϕ(n)bi≡∏i=1ϕ(n)bi(modn)aϕ(n)≡1(modn)\begin{gather*} \prod_{i = 1}^{\phi(n)} ab_i &\equiv \prod_{i = 1}^{\phi(n)} b_i \pmod{n} \\ a^{\phi(n)} \prod_{i = 1}^{\phi(n)} b_i &\equiv \prod_{i = 1}^{\phi(n)} b_i \pmod{n} \\ a^{\phi(n)} &\equiv 1 \pmod{n} \end{gather*}i=1∏ϕ(n)​abi​aϕ(n)i=1∏ϕ(n)​bi​aϕ(n)​≡i=1∏ϕ(n)​bi​(modn)≡i=1∏ϕ(n)​bi​(modn)≡1(modn)​

Fermat’s Little Theorem

Fermat’s Little Theorem states that if ppp is a prime and aaa is a positive integer that is not divisible by ppp, then

ap−1≡1(modp)a^{p - 1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}ap−1≡1(modp)

Proof

Since ppp is a prime, ϕ(p)=p−1\phi(p) = p - 1ϕ(p)=p−1.

Since aaa is less than ppp and ppp is a prime, aaa must be coprime to ppp.

Apply Euler’s Theorem and get ap−1≡1(modp)a^{p - 1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}ap−1≡1(modp).

□\square□

Wilson’s Theorem

Wilson’s Theorem states that

(p−1)!≡−1(modp)  ⟺  p is prime(p - 1)! \equiv -1 \pmod{p} \iff p \text{ is prime}(p−1)!≡−1(modp)⟺p is prime

where ppp is an integer greater than 111.

Proof

We need to prove two things:

  1. p is prime  ⟹  (p−1)!≡−1(modp)p \text{ is prime} \implies (p - 1)! \equiv -1 \pmod{p}p is prime⟹(p−1)!≡−1(modp)
  2. (p−1)!≡−1(modp)  ⟹  p is prime(p - 1)! \equiv -1 \pmod{p} \implies p \text{ is prime}(p−1)!≡−1(modp)⟹p is prime

1. p is prime  ⟹  (p−1)!≡−1(modp)p \text{ is prime} \implies (p - 1)! \equiv -1 \pmod{p}p is prime⟹(p−1)!≡−1(modp)

When p=2p = 2p=2, (p−1)!=1≡−1(modp)(p - 1)! = 1 \equiv -1 \pmod{p}(p−1)!=1≡−1(modp), which is obviously true, so we are only going to prove the case where ppp is an odd prime.

We are going to prove a lemma first.

Lemma 1. x2≡1(modp)x ^ 2 \equiv 1 \pmod{p}x2≡1(modp) has only 2 solutions where ppp is a odd prime, and they are 111 and p−1p - 1p−1

Proof

x2≡1(modp)x2−1≡0(modp)(x−1)(x+1)≡0(modp)\begin{gather*} x ^ 2 \equiv 1 \pmod{p} \\ x ^ 2 - 1 \equiv 0 \pmod{p} \\ (x - 1)(x + 1) \equiv 0 \pmod{p} \end{gather*}x2≡1(modp)x2−1≡0(modp)(x−1)(x+1)≡0(modp)​

Since ppp is a prime, p∣(x−1)(x+1)p | (x - 1)(x + 1)p∣(x−1)(x+1) gives p∣x−1p | x - 1p∣x−1 or p∣x+1p | x + 1p∣x+1.

Therefore, the only solutions are 111 and p−1p - 1p−1.

□\square□

Then we go back to the proof of Wilson’s Theorem.

Consider Euler’s Theorem’s proof. We know that {ab1,ab2,…,abϕ(n)}\{a b_1, a b_2, \dots, a b_{\phi(n)}\}{ab1​,ab2​,…,abϕ(n)​} is a permutation of BBB, which gives the inverse of aaa modulo nnn is unique if nnn is prime and 0<a<n0 < a < n0<a<n.

Thus, we can pair up each number in BBB with its inverse modulo ppp except 111 and p−1p - 1p−1, whose inverse is themselves.

Therefore, we have

∏i=1p−1i≡1⋅(p−1)≡−1(modp)(p−1)!≡−1(modp)\begin{gather*} \prod_{i = 1}^{p - 1} i \equiv 1 \cdot (p - 1) \equiv -1 \pmod{p} \\ (p - 1)! \equiv -1 \pmod{p} \end{gather*} i=1∏p−1​i≡1⋅(p−1)≡−1(modp)(p−1)!≡−1(modp)​

□\square□

2. (p−1)!≡−1(modp)  ⟹  p is prime(p - 1)! \equiv -1 \pmod{p} \implies p \text{ is prime}(p−1)!≡−1(modp)⟹p is prime

We want to prove that non-prime ppp can’t satisfy (p−1)!≡−1(modp)(p - 1)! \equiv -1 \pmod{p}(p−1)!≡−1(modp).

Then minimum integer ppp that is not a prime is 444, so we start from p=4p = 4p=4.

When p>=4p >= 4p>=4, (p−1)!≡(p−1)(p−2)!(p - 1)! \equiv (p - 1)(p - 2)!(p−1)!≡(p−1)(p−2)!

Since ppp is composite, let its minimum divisor be ddd.

Then d≤p≤p−2d \leq \sqrt{p} \leq p - 2d≤p​≤p−2.

We obtain (p−2)!≡d⋅(p−2)!d(modp)(p - 2)! \equiv d \cdot \dfrac{(p - 2)!}{d} \pmod{p}(p−2)!≡d⋅d(p−2)!​(modp).

Since ddd has no inverse modulo ppp (by applying CRT), we have d⋅(p−2)!d≢1(modp)d \cdot \dfrac{(p - 2)!}{d} \not\equiv 1 \pmod{p}d⋅d(p−2)!​≡1(modp).

Therefore, (p−1)!≡(p−1)(p−2)!≢−1(modp)(p - 1)! \equiv (p - 1)(p - 2)! \not\equiv -1 \pmod{p}(p−1)!≡(p−1)(p−2)!≡−1(modp).

□\square□
